# Internal AI Usage Analysis

**Maturity and Approach:** Teneo’s internal adoption of AI is in the **early-to-mid stages of maturity**, characterized by recent strategic planning, initial tooling efforts, and leadership-driven experimentation. The firm only established a formal internal AI initiative in late 2024/2025 with the appointment of a head of AI and the recruitment of engineers ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-appoints-ryan-cox-as-global-head-of-ai/#:~:text=continuing%20to%20develop%20a%20network,in%20productivity%20and%20knowledge%20sharing)) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/careers/open-positions/7317848003/#:~:text=,grade)). This suggests that 2023 was largely exploratory (with general analytics use), and serious internal development began in 2025. Evidence points to a **top-down approach**: senior management mandates an AI strategy, and a small dedicated team is building capabilities, rather than a grassroots or long-standing tech program.

- **Verified Internal Initiatives:** The clearest verified internal initiative is the **development of an internal AI platform and workflows**. Job descriptions from late 2025 explicitly mention creating AI workflows, a data lake, and leveraging cloud AI services (Azure) to automate internal processes ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/careers/open-positions/7317848003/#:~:text=,grade)) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/careers/open-positions/7317848003/#:~:text=professional%20software%20engineering%20experience%20with,identity%2C%20RBAC%2C%20secrets)). - **Likely Internal Use-Cases:** Based on Teneo’s business, **internal AI use cases** probably include: 
  - *Research and Intelligence:* Using AI to scan and summarize news, financial reports, or social media to support Teneo’s advisory work (akin to an internal analyst co-pilot).

  - *Operational Efficiency:* Automation of routine internal workflows – possibly in IT (e.g., AI for helpdesk or data classification) or HR (e.g., resume screening, although that’s speculative). Given Teneo’s focus, more likely in **analytics**: automating data gathering and analysis for client projects (like building dashboards with AI assistance).

**Bottom line (internal use):** So far, **verified facts** show Teneo has put in place the *people* and *plans* for internal AI usage (leadership mandate, partnerships for tech, initial team hiring) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-appoints-ryan-cox-as-global-head-of-ai/#:~:text=continuing%20to%20develop%20a%20network,in%20productivity%20and%20knowledge%20sharing)) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/careers/open-positions/7317848003/#:~:text=,grade)). **Strong inference** from these is that initial tools (like AI-assisted research and automation) are under development now, focusing on augmenting employee capabilities and streamlining analysis. **Speculative inference** is that Teneo will end up with a suite of internal AI-enabled knowledge tools (possibly an internal conversational assistant tapping their data, and templates for AI-generated content) – but evidence for these specific outcomes will have to be observed in the coming year. At this point, Teneo’s internal AI adoption is earnest but in **“build mode,”** with tangible benefits yet to be publicly demonstrated.

  - That said, if a particular AI partnership became extremely critical and successful, Teneo might consider acquiring or more tightly integrating it in the future. (No indication of that yet – e.g., Teneo did not take a stake in Aily that we know of).
  
- **Partnership Outcomes:** We should consider how these partnerships position Teneo in the market:
  - The Aily partnership gives Teneo a **talking point at the table with clients**: “We have an AI tool that can crunch your numbers with 99% accuracy.” Even if clients don’t use it, it shows Teneo is proactive.
  - The Thoughtworks JV raised Teneo’s profile; being featured in Axios suggests a splash that “Teneo is doing something innovative in AI.” It can attract potential clients who read that and think Teneo can handle AI projects.
  - These moves also likely help **recruitment and retention**: consultants at Teneo see that the firm is doing exciting AI stuff, making it an attractive place to work on cutting-edge issues.
  
- **Risks in Partnerships:** Of course, relying on partners means:
  - Teneo must coordinate cross-company teams, which can be challenging.
  - They must ensure quality and integration – if an AI solution built by the JV fails or has issues, Teneo’s reputation is on the line just as much as Thoughtworks’.
  - There’s also competition: Big consultancies partnering with OpenAI or others, but Teneo’s advantage is agility and the combined model rather than exclusivity.
  
In conclusion, **partnerships are fundamental to Teneo’s AI strategy**. The firm systematically identified what it’s missing (tech platforms, engineering capacity, predictive analytics engines) and went out to partner for those. Each partnership serves a distinct need – Aily for intelligent analytics, Thoughtworks for custom development, cloud providers for scalable AI infrastructure – collectively enabling Teneo to deliver a full suite of AI advisory and implementation without building a large tech arm from scratch. This ecosystem approach is a prudent strategy for a firm of Teneo’s size and background, allowing it to punch above its weight in AI consulting by leveraging the strengths of specialized collaborators.

They likely want to be seen as *thought leaders in AI’s implications for business*, integrated with all the other strategic issues CEOs face.

In conclusion, Teneo’s market positioning around AI is that of the **strategic navigator** for AI in the corporate world – they present themselves as the firm that understands both the transformative potential of AI and the pragmatic steps and guardrails needed to realize that potential. Their messaging is tuned to resonate with CEOs who are hungry for AI opportunities but wary of the risks and complexities. By consistently using language about strategy, leadership, ROI, and alignment (and avoiding technical jargon or grandiose tech promises), Teneo stays in its lane as a trusted advisor, while still embracing the cutting-edge nature of AI. This careful balance appears to be a deliberate strategy to make Teneo synonymous with **“smart AI adoption”** in the advisory realm.

# Bottom-Line Assessment

**Teneo’s AI posture** since 2023 is one of an evolving but increasingly **serious institutional commitment** to integrate AI into both its client services and its own operations:

- **Strategic Priority, Top-Down Driven:** AI has clearly become a strategic priority for Teneo, championed by the CEO and Board. This is evidenced by the creation of a Global Head of AI role reporting to top management ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-appoints-ryan-cox-as-global-head-of-ai/#:~:text=,%E2%80%9D)), the public statements by CEO Paul Keary and Chair Ursula Burns on AI’s importance ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/ceo-and-investor-confidence-remains-strong-heading-into-2026-despite-global-headwinds/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CAI%20spending%20is%20set%20to,%E2%80%9D)), and resource investments like the Thoughtworks JV. The pattern suggests that Teneo’s leadership views AI as a transformative force in the advisory industry and is allocating significant attention and capital to not fall behind.

- **Client Offerings Moving from Advisory-Only to Advisory+Execution:** Initially, Teneo approached AI by advising clients through thought leadership and pointing out strategic considerations (e.g., “be mindful of ROI expectations, talent needs” etc.). Over 2024-2025, it has progressed to offering concrete solutions – *partnering with Aily Labs to deliver an analytics tool, and by 2026, co-owning a venture to build AI products for clients* ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-announces-ai-partnership-with-aily-labs-to-transform-enterprise-decision-making-for-global-ceos/#:~:text=Teneo%2C%20the%20global%20CEO%20advisory,powered%20decision%20intelligence)) ([www.axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/16/ai-business-tools-teneo-thoughtworks#:~:text=Zoom%20in%3A%20The%20venture%20combines,Keary%2C%20Teneo%27s%20CEO%2C%20told%20Axios)). This trajectory shows that Teneo is transitioning from just telling clients *“what to do about AI”* towards also *“helping them do it.”* While Teneo itself is not writing AI code at scale, it orchestrates those who do, thereby expanding its value proposition. This indicates a **maturing capability**: still emerging in execution, but no longer purely conceptual.

- **Internal Adoption is Underway, but Results Not Yet Visible:** Internally, Teneo has put foundational pieces in place (leadership roles, strategy plans, initial tech infrastructure via Azure, a nascent AI engineering team) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-appoints-ryan-cox-as-global-head-of-ai/#:~:text=continuing%20to%20develop%20a%20network,in%20productivity%20and%20knowledge%20sharing)) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/careers/open-positions/7317848003/#:~:text=,grade)). However, evidence of concrete outcomes (like proprietary internal AI tools or significant efficiency gains achieved) is thin so far – understandable given these efforts started in earnest only in late 2024/2025. The **inference** is that Teneo likely has pilot internal projects (maybe an AI knowledge search tool or automated research assistant) in testing, but these have not been publicized. We can say Teneo is *“walking the talk”* by trying to embed AI internally, but it’s still in early implementation stages with respect to measurable impact.

- **Capabilities and Credibility Building:** Teneo has bolstered its AI capabilities primarily through *external partnerships and selective hiring*. It did not have a legacy of tech development, so it wisely leveraged partners (Aily for analytics, Thoughtworks for build, big tech for infrastructure) ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-announces-ai-partnership-with-aily-labs-to-transform-enterprise-decision-making-for-global-ceos/#:~:text=Teneo%20brings%20to%20the%20partnership,time%2C%20actionable%20insights)) ([www.axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/16/ai-business-tools-teneo-thoughtworks#:~:text=%2A%20The%20AI,Mike%20Sutcliff%2C%20CEO%20of%20Thoughtworks)). With Ryan Cox on board and a handful of tech hires, Teneo has the nucleus of an AI competency, but it’s still a **lean model** (the heavy lifting on tech is done by collaborators). This approach is credible for now – it allows Teneo to offer sophisticated AI advice/solutions without needing hundreds of engineers. However, it also means Teneo’s own intellectual property in AI remains limited (no proprietary platform yet). The firm’s credibility in AI comes from its people (Cox’s expertise, thought leadership evidence) and partner associations, rather than in-house-developed tech assets.

- **Market Differentiation:** Teneo is carving out a niche as the adviser who sits between the corporate leadership and the tech implementers in AI. All communications reinforce that niche – *bridging boardrooms and AI labs* ([www.axios.com](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/16/ai-business-tools-teneo-thoughtworks#:~:text=What%20they%27re%20saying%3A%20,lead%20the%20venture%2C%20told%20Axios)). This positioning seems well-defined and consistent. It likely resonates with CEOs who may not want to go to a pure IT consultancy for strategic advice. By not directly competing with large IT service providers and instead partnering, Teneo avoids a head-to-head on technical depth and focuses on strategic value and integration. The bottom-line is that Teneo is betting that **AI advisory needs a combination of strategy, communications, and tech** – a combination it believes plays to its strengths.

- **Evidence Gaps and Watching Forward:** There are areas where evidence is still **limited or developing**:
  - We have no direct case studies or client testimonials to prove Teneo’s AI advisory has delivered results – this is likely because projects are either confidential or ongoing. Over time, if Teneo’s AI engagements succeed, we may see references in their materials (even anonymized success metrics).
  - It’s unclear how deep the AI knowledge runs within the broader consultant pool at Teneo. Aside from a few named experts and authors, has Teneo trained many of its ~1,600 staff on AI tools, or is expertise concentrated in a small core team? The internal adoption statements are high-level; details on training programs or internal AI governance remain internal. The **inference** is that this is still in progress – a next step for Teneo would be upskilling more of its consultants to be “AI fluent” in engagements.
  - No mention of AI in Teneo’s **revenue or offering breakdown** is public – we don’t know if Teneo has started booking significant fees for AI-related services, or if this is still mostly an investment area. Given the timeline, AI-related revenue is probably nascent. The seriousness of commitment (like the JV) suggests they expect it to grow.

- **Commitment Level:** All signals (executive engagement, dedicated hiring, new venture creation, partnership network, intensive thought leadership focus) point to a **high institutional commitment** to AI, not a passing experiment. Especially the launch of a JV with a major firm and the framing of moves as “continued strategic investment” ([www.teneo.com](https://www.teneo.com/news/press-releases/teneo-appoints-ryan-cox-as-global-head-of-ai/#:~:text=,%E2%80%9D)) indicate this is part of Teneo’s long-term strategy. In less than three years, Teneo went from barely mentioning AI to making it a core theme in their identity. That steep ramp-up shows responsiveness to market demand and a proactive attempt to reposition the firm for the future.

- **Risks/Challenges:** Teneo will need to ensure it can deliver on the expectations it’s setting. For instance, promising that they can help turn AI ambition into reality means they must manage successful outcomes via the Thoughtworks JV – a realm somewhat new to them. There’s a risk if these projects falter (e.g., if custom AI tools don’t live up to hype, Teneo could face credibility issues). Also, bigger consulting competitors are all-in on AI; Teneo must leverage its agility and C-suite focus to compete. So far, Teneo is differentiating on quality of counsel, but it cannot rest – it likely will need to continue investing, perhaps even consider acquiring niche talent or tech if needed to keep up.

**Bottom line:** Teneo has moved assertively to embrace AI as a core part of its advisory offering, aligning with how its CEO clients’ needs are evolving. The firm appears to be successfully *positioning itself as an expert guide* in the AI revolution for business leaders, backed by data-driven insights and partnerships that give it execution capability. While many of these initiatives are recent and yet to fully mature, the pattern of activities – from high-level thought leadership to concrete partnerships and the standing up of a new AI-focused team – strongly suggests that Teneo is not treating AI as a buzzword add-on, but as a fundamental component of its growth and service strategy in the years ahead.
