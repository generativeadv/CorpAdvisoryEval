# Section 7: Positioning and Messaging  

Brunswick’s market narrative around AI is carefully crafted to align with its identity as a strategic communications and issues management firm. They frame AI not as a purely technical revolution, but as a **multi-dimensional business challenge** that requires the kind of counsel Brunswick provides. Several key themes recur in Brunswick’s messaging:

- **AI as a “Critical Issue”:** Brunswick consistently places AI in the context of critical corporate issues akin to regulatory change, cyber risk, or ESG. On their homepage and materials, Brunswick describes itself as advising on “critical issues,” and AI is depicted as exactly that – a transformative issue that can define reputations. In Brunswick Review articles, AI is often mentioned in the same breath as other board-level concerns (e.g., sustainability, geopolitics) ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/issue/ai-impact/#:~:text=)). This positioning means Brunswick treats AI as a boardroom topic where their strategic counsel is needed, rather than a niche IT topic.

- **Opportunity & Risk Balance:** Brunswick’s messaging hits a balanced tone: **optimistic about AI’s potential, yet frank about its risks.** For example, Brunswick’s content like “Business + AI + X” explicitly talks about AI’s major impact on global challenges, implying opportunities for innovation ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/business-ai-x/#:~:text=AI%20will%20have%E2%80%94and%20is%20already,By%20Jon%20Miller)). At the same time, their pieces discuss pitfalls like misinformation (AI-generated fake news) and the trust deficit if AI is mismanaged ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/issue/ai-impact/#:~:text=Misinformation%20%26%20Disinformation)) ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/investor-survey-2026/#:~:text=But%20they%E2%80%99re%20not%20blind%20to,use%20AI%20to%20update%20their)). The tagline isn’t fearmongering nor blindly celebratory; it’s “AI is powerful – you need to handle it wisely.” This positions Brunswick as the sober advisor who helps clients maximize AI’s upside while mitigating downsides.

- **Straplines and Language:** Brunswick doesn’t use a single tagline for AI, but phrases about *“future-forward solutions”*, *“cutting through the noise”*, and *“talent and technology”* blending together are prevalent ([www.brunswickgroup.com](https://www.brunswickgroup.com/practices/leadership-presence-visibility/#:~:text=research%2C%20creativity%2C%20and%20digital%20%26,and%20create%20lasting%2C%20meaningful%20impact)). This language appears on their site under digital/creative offerings, indicating they sell the idea that they combine human insight with AI capability. Another interesting phrase is “**Algorithmic Relations**” ([www.brunswickgroup.com](https://www.brunswickgroup.com/practices/leadership-presence-visibility/#:~:text=match%20at%20L278%20,Misinformation%20%26%20Disinformation)) – likely Brunswick’s term for managing relationships with audiences in an age when algorithms (social media feeds, search engines, AI assistants) determine what those audiences see. By coining such a term, Brunswick signals it understands the altered communications landscape AI brings. Similarly, including “**Misinformation & Disinformation**” as a service line item acknowledges AI’s role in those threats, signalling Brunswick can help defend against them.

- **Not Just Tech – Stakeholder Impact:** The messaging places AI in contexts of corporate affairs, public policy, and societal impact. They raise points about AI in sustainability (AI’s energy use vs. its benefits) ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/issue/ai-impact/#:~:text=)), AI in workforce (though not explicitly in sources, presumably addressing employee concerns), and AI in regulation (global competition and the need for governance) ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/leitmotif-cofounder/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20US%20is%20great%20in,%E2%80%9D)). By doing so, Brunswick positions its AI advice as part of larger narratives: e.g., *“How do we ensure our AI initiatives align with our corporate purpose and social responsibilities?”* – a question Brunswick is priming itself to answer.

- **Geopolitical/Competitive Framing:** Some of Brunswick’s messaging frames AI as a race or competition – for example, discussing whether the U.S. will win the AI race ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/issue/ai-impact/#:~:text=AI)). This resonates with C-suite fears of falling behind. Brunswick can thus pitch services around benchmarking a client’s positioning on AI against peers, or navigating the East-West tech decoupling issues. It shows Brunswick isn’t just looking at AI in isolation but as a factor in global strategy, which is a higher-level framing that appeals to CEOs and boards.

- **Reputational Lens:** Crucially, Brunswick’s narrative always brings AI back to **reputation, trust, and communication**. Whether it’s advising that companies should “trumpet their competitive advantage” in AI to investors ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/investor-survey-2026/#:~:text=Companies%20eager%20to%20attract%20investment,they%20most%20want%20to%20hear)) or warning that *if AI summarizes your info, you better have that info right* ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/investor-survey-2026/#:~:text=responsibility%20for%20how%20information%20reaches,more%20frequently%20than%20mainstream%20media)), the focus is on how AI intersects with perception. Their public commentary essentially tells companies: *AI will change how you are seen – by investors, media, employees – and we’ll help you manage that.* This is very much in line with Brunswick’s core messaging of shaping and protecting reputations.

- **Tone and style:** The tone Brunswick uses in AI discussions is **authoritative and policy-aware**. It’s not technical jargon-laden; it reads more like analysis you’d see in The Economist or Foreign Affairs. For instance, Caroline Daniel’s piece on Chief AI Officers contextualizes the rise of that role across sectors ([review.brunswickgroup.com](https://review.brunswickgroup.com/article/chief-ai-officer/#:~:text=Brunswick%E2%80%99s%20Caroline%20Daniel%20talks%20to,the%20new%20senior%20executive%20role)), talking about NASA to L’Oréal – showing range and a bit of storytelling, not coding details. This style reinforces Brunswick’s image as seasoned counselors who understand business, technology, and policy all at once. It’s likely quite intentional to differentiate from pure tech firms: Brunswick speaks the language of CEOs and regulators about AI, not the language of developers.

- **Peripheral vs Central:** Is AI central or peripheral in Brunswick’s overall narrative? Based on 2025 content, AI has moved toward the center. The dedicated issue of their magazine and integration into service descriptions suggest Brunswick is increasingly putting AI front and center as a theme. However, it’s still one of many critical issues – Brunswick also talks about climate, DE&I, activism, etc., as core issues. AI is becoming a core part of their narrative but framed as part of a broader portfolio of expertise. For example, a Brunswick homepage might simultaneously highlight “sustainability, cybersecurity, and AI” as domains they can help with. AI is not (and likely will not be) the single defining banner like it might be for a tech consultancy, but it’s absolutely a key pillar of their current positioning.

In summary, Brunswick’s market messaging casts the firm as a **trusted guide in the AI era**, one who brings clarity and strategy to the chaos of rapid technological change. They avoid hyperbole, instead using strategic language about trust, value, and preparedness. This messaging serves to reassure clients that Brunswick is equipped to advise on AI implications and to attract business from those anxious about how AI affects their stakeholder landscape. It’s a classic Brunswick approach: place themselves at the crossroads of technological change and stakeholder expectation, and communicate that effectively to the market.

The current evidence is that they are laying the groundwork for such repeatable tools, even if today most deliverables are bespoke analyses. Where evidence is **thin** is mainly around Brunswick’s client success stories or case studies in AI. We infer capabilities and intentions, but we have limited public confirmation of, say, “Brunswick helped X company navigate an AI-related communications crisis with Y result.” This is not surprising, as Brunswick often operates discreetly. Nevertheless, it means some of Brunswick’s AI posture remains implied rather than openly proven. There’s also little explicit detail on how Brunswick governs AI use (no public responsible AI policy), which could be an omission or simply kept internal. Additionally, while Brunswick is clearly doing a lot with AI content, we don’t see evidence of *partnership-based* moves (no alliances or acquisitions in AI), which could imply that Brunswick is focusing inward and might be somewhat behind firms that have, for instance, acquired data companies – or it could simply reflect Brunswick’s preference for organic capability growth.

The **pattern and momentum** strongly suggest a serious institutional commitment rather than a fad or marketing ploy. Over 2023–2025, Brunswick moved from relatively few mentions of AI to making it a centerpiece of their flagship publications and a notable element of their hiring criteria. Such sustained effort – from top leadership messaging to ground-level tool adoption – indicates that Brunswick’s leadership views AI as integral to staying competitive and delivering value in the communications advisory market. In essence, Brunswick is ensuring it will not be left behind as AI changes how stories are told and reputations are managed. They are doing so in a way that fits their brand: quietly building capability and thought leadership, focusing on how AI intersects with human judgment and strategic decision-making. 

For a board evaluating competitive positions, the bottom line is: **Brunswick is taking AI seriously, investing in talent and tools, and weaving AI into its core communications advisory services.** It may not shout about AI as loudly as tech-forward consultancies, but the factual evidence shows a clear trajectory of adoption and innovation. Clients can expect Brunswick to bring AI-informed insights and counsel to the table, though Brunswick will frame it as part of holistic advice (not a tech solution in isolation). In conclusion, Brunswick’s AI posture is one of a firm in transformation – evolving its traditional strengths with new technology – and the evidence to date points to steady, institutionally backed progress on that journey.
